Life People Parties

Life

Residence

Jay Gatsby’s house was a sprawling mansion that stood as a monument to his wealth and dreams, an extravagant display of opulence designed to captivate and impress. Located in West Egg, it had all the grandeur of a European palace, with towering spires, marble floors, and grand staircases. Outside, the estate boasted vibrant gardens, a luxurious swimming pool,

and a marble patio overlooking the bay, where the green light from Daisy’s dock flickered in the distance. During Gatsby's legendary parties, the mansion came to life with music, laughter, and a steady flow of champagne, transforming into a surreal world of endless celebration—yet, beneath the glamour, it felt strangely hollow, as if all the luxury existed only to mask Gatsby’s loneliness.

Career

Jay Gatsby’s career as a bootlegger was his path to quickly amassing a large sum of wealth. He partnered with shady figures like Meyer Wolfsheim, who guided him through the illegal liquor trade during Prohibition, enabling Gatsby to turn a

significant profit selling alcohol on the black market. Though outwardly he presented himself as a successful businessman, his fortune was tainted by the criminal underworld, hidden behind the glamorous facade of his mansion and lavish parties.

Rumours and Lies

The guests at Jay Gatsby’s lavish parties, spread endless rumors about his past and fortune. Some whispered that he was a German spy during the war, while others insisted he had killed a man. There were tales that he was the nephew or cousin of Kaiser Wilhelm, suggesting his wealth had sinister origins. Others speculated that he was simply a successful bootlegger, attributing his fortune to illegal liquor sales. These rumors, swirling among people who barely knew him, only added to Gatsby’s allure and mystique, making him an almost mythical figure.

Jay Gatsby told several lies about himself to craft an identity that would impress Daisy and fit into the world of old money. He claimed to be the son of wealthy, deceased parents from the Midwest. He also spun a tale of attending Oxford. Gatsby further fabricated his life by telling people that he had inherited his wealth. These lies were part of his carefully constructed persona, designed to make him appear worthy of the life he dreamed of with Daisy.

Truth

Jay Gatsby, born James Gatz to poor farmers in North Dakota, was determined to escape his humble beginnings. He reinvented himself after meeting wealthy copper magnate Dan Cody, who mentored him and gave him a taste of luxury. When Cody died, Gatsby was left with nothing but used his

newfound ambition to enter the world of organized crime, eventually becoming a bootlegger. His wealth, built through illegal means, allowed him to purchase a mansion in West Egg, where he threw lavish parties in hopes of winning back Daisy Buchanan, the woman who symbolized the life he longed for.
